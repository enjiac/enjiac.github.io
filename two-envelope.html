Two Envelopes and Poker

There are two envelopes on the table. You are told that one of them contains double the amount of money as the other. Say you open one and find $20, the other envelope therefore has either $10 or $40. The question is would you swap your envelope for the other one on the table.

Since it is equally likely that you picked the envelope with less money or more money, there should be a 50% chance of $10 in the other envelope and a 50% chance of $40. The expected value of the other envelope is 0.5*10 + 0.5*40 = 25, which is larger than the 20 you have on hand. You should switch. But this conclusion applies to all situations. No matter what amount you find in the first envelope you picked, it seems like you should always switch to the other envelope. But how can your first choice always be sub-optimal? This is the two-envelop paradox.
