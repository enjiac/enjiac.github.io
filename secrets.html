Finding Secrets

In his book Zero to One, Peter Thiel poses the question "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" Thiel calls a correct answer to the question a "secret" — a piece of unpopular/unconventional truth. 

Secrets create superior returns in markets. If everyone looks at a company with a similar narrative, fixated on the same growth drivers, the same risks, they would arrive at a similar opinion. I see this phenomenon in some research reports, where the research talks about the issues and guidance management highlighted and put on a multiple to get to valuation, often vaguely justified by industry peers or historical averages, and acts more as a reaction to recent price movements than an effort to anticipate the future. To generate great returns we need a secret — a differentiated, idiosyncratic way of looking at the world and assessing what lies in the future of a company. Secrets explain anomalies and break the efficient market theory. 

I don't think I will find secrets by studying finance but rather find them somewhere in the humanities. Philosophy is an interesting field because we learn about independent thinkers in the past who suggested new ways of understanding people — our motivations, needs, and desires. 

Another way to find secrets is through first principles thinking, to reason from fundamental truths rather than automatically accept beliefs held by most people.
